# Professional English

## Summary

This note organizes professional English reminders for speaking and writing more clearly. The main goals are to reduce filler words, choose more precise vocabulary, avoid common word-confusion mistakes, and practice smoother workplace speech.

## Key Points

- Stop using filler words when they do not add meaning.
- Replace vague positive words like "awesome" with more precise adjectives.
- Practice confusing word pairs because they can change meaning completely.
- Work on pronunciation, linking, and intonation so speech sounds smoother.
- Review vocabulary by rewriting simple sentences in a more professional way.

## Filler Words And Cleaner Rephrasing

Some words are not always wrong, but they become filler when they are used without a clear purpose. The goal is not to sound complicated. The goal is to sound direct, calm, and precise.

| Pattern | Why it can weaken the sentence | Better habit | Example |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| like | Often used as a pause instead of meaning "similar to" | Pause silently or choose the exact word | Instead of "It was like difficult," say "It was difficult." |
| approximately | Useful only when giving an estimate | Use it only for numbers, dates, or measurements | "Approximately 20 people attended" is clear. "The task was approximately hard" is not natural. |
| kind of / sort of | Makes the idea sound uncertain | Say the direct adjective if you are sure | "The design is unclear" instead of "The design is kind of unclear." |
| basically | Often adds no meaning | Remove it unless you are summarizing | "We need to finish testing" instead of "Basically, we need to finish testing." |
| actually | Can sound defensive or unnecessary | Use only when correcting a misunderstanding | "The meeting starts at 2" instead of "Actually, the meeting starts at 2." |
| just | Can make a request sound smaller than it is | Remove it in professional requests | "I wanted to check the timeline" instead of "I just wanted to check the timeline." |

## Filler Word Practice Workflow

1. Write or record what you want to say naturally.
2. Ask ChatGPT to identify filler words and vague phrases.
3. Rewrite the sentence without filler words.
4. Replace vague words with precise vocabulary.
5. Say the cleaner version out loud until it sounds natural.

## Commonly Confused Words

| Word pair | Correct meaning | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| advice / advise | Advice is a noun. Advise is a verb. | "Advice" is non-countable, so say "pieces of advice" instead of "advices." |
| moral / morale | Moral means principles of right and wrong. Morale means the mood or spirit of a group. | "Team morale is high" means the group feels positive. |
| career / carrier | Career means profession. Carrier means something or someone that transports. | "My career is in software" versus "The airline is a carrier." |
| silicon / silicone | Silicon is a chemical element used in technology. Silicone is a flexible synthetic material. | Silicon Valley uses "silicon," not "silicone." |
| pronounce / pronunciation | Pronounce is the verb. Pronunciation is the noun. | Do not say "pronounciation." |
| anyway / anyways | Anyway is the standard form. | "Anyways" is casual and often sounds less polished. |

## Replacing "Awesome"

"Awesome" is fine in casual conversation, but it can be too vague in professional settings. Choose the replacement based on what you actually mean.

| Alternative | Best use |
| --- | --- |
| amazing | General positive reaction |
| brilliant | Smart idea or excellent solution |
| astonishing | Very surprising or impressive |
| exceptional | Much better than average |
| fabulous | Warm, positive, and expressive |
| magnificent | Grand, beautiful, or impressive |
| marvelous | Wonderful or impressive |
| outstanding | Excellent performance or result |
| remarkable | Worth noticing because it is unusual or impressive |
| striking | Visually or emotionally noticeable |

## Workplace Phrasal Verbs

| Phrasal verb | Meaning | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| pay off | To produce good results after effort, or to finish paying a loan | The extra practice paid off. |
| lay off | To lose a job because of company cutbacks | The company laid off several employees. |
| wrap up | To finish a task or meeting completely | Let us wrap up the meeting. |
| burn out | To become exhausted from overworking | She burned out after months of overtime. |
| miss out | To lose an opportunity by not taking action | I do not want to miss out on that opportunity. |
| price out | To become unable to afford something | Many buyers were priced out of the market. |
| call in | To notify work that you cannot come in | He called in sick this morning. |

## Useful Workplace Phrases

| Situation | Natural phrase | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Starting collaboration | I am looking forward to working with you. | Complete and professional. |
| Starting collaboration with a group | I am looking forward to working together. | Natural for teams. |
| Ending a positive message | I am excited to contribute to the project. | More specific than "sounds awesome." |
| Showing appreciation | Thank you for the helpful feedback. | Clearer than only saying "thanks." |

## Speaking Habits

| Habit | What to practice |
| --- | --- |
| Avoid uptalk | Do not raise your voice at the end of statements unless you are asking a real question. |
| Use linking | Linking is not speaking faster. It makes speech smoother by connecting sounds naturally. |
| Pause instead of filling space | A short pause sounds more confident than repeated filler words. |
| Repeat the cleaned sentence | After removing filler words, say the sentence again until it feels natural. |

## Native Workplace Expressions

Use these expressions to replace direct or non-native sounding phrases with more natural workplace English.

| Direct expression | Native expression | Meaning | Example |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| not good quality work | subpar work | Work below expectations | The report was subpar and needs revisions. |
| in that profession | in the field | In that industry or profession | She has 10 years of experience in the field. |
| a lot of different skills | a diverse skill set | Many useful abilities | He has a diverse skill set in engineering and management. |
| got a better job / got promoted | worked his way up | Gradually advanced in a company | He worked his way up from intern to manager. |
| this needs to be done right away | this is time-sensitive | Needs quick attention | This document is time-sensitive, so please review it today. |
| Did you understand my idea? | Did I get my point across? | Did I explain clearly? | I hope I got my point across during the meeting. |
| makes a lot of money | high-grossing | Generates a lot of revenue | That was one of the highest-grossing movies of the year. |
| Did the boss approve the budget? | Did the boss sign off on the budget? | Formally approve something | We cannot move forward until she signs off on the budget. |
| in advance | upfront / up front | Before something happens | We need to pay part of the cost upfront. |
| spend less money | cut costs | Reduce expenses | The company is trying to cut costs this quarter. |
| becoming very successful | taking off | Growing quickly or becoming successful | Their new product is really taking off. |
| existed for 30 years | has been around for 30 years | Has existed for a long time | This company has been around for 30 years. |
| talks to people easily | has good people skills | Communicates well with others | She is great with clients because she has strong people skills. |
| not determined yet | up in the air | Still undecided | Our launch date is still up in the air. |
| let's continue with the original idea | stick to the plan | Continue with the original plan | We should stick to the plan unless something changes. |
| changed their mind and left the project | backed out of the project | Withdrew from something | The vendor backed out of the project last week. |
| let's start / don't delay | let's get going | Start now | We have a lot to cover, so let's get going. |
| I quit my job | I gave my notice | Formally told employer you are leaving | I gave my notice last Friday. |
| I worked every second Saturday | I worked every other Saturday | One Saturday yes, next Saturday no | I used to work every other Saturday. |
| we are not improving | we are not getting anywhere | Not making progress | We have discussed this for an hour, but we are not getting anywhere. |
| we are improving | we are getting somewhere | Making progress | Now we are getting somewhere. |
